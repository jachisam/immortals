About the Film

“When this world was still young, long before man or beast roamed these lands, there was a war in the heavens. Immortals, once thoughts incapable of death, discovered they had the power to kill one another. Lost in this war was a weapon of unimaginable power, the Epirus bow. The victors declared themselves Gods, while the vanquished were renamed Titans and forever imprisoned within the bowels of Mount Tartarus. Eons passed, mankind flourished and the great war receded from memory. But the evil that once was has reemerged.”

Immortals weaves the story of one King Hyperion, whose apostasy has left him intoxicated with the power of war against humanity. Amassing a savage army of hoplites personally disfigured by the bloodthirsty king himself, Hyperion has scorched and plundered Greece in search of the legendary Epirus Bow, a weapon forged by Ares himself—a weapon so powerful, it has the power to unleash the Titans from the pits of Tartarus and annihilate the Olympians, who met his prayers with silence as Hyperion’s family fell sick and died. The Olympians are subject to ancient law, which forbids them from intervening in mortal affairs—until a peasant named Theseus is chosen by Zeus as the last hope for both the gods and for humanity. Along with his band of outsiders—including the oracular priestess Phaedra, and the Machiavellian slave Stavros—Theseus must defeat Hyperion and his forces, or watch his homeland fall to the Titans and his Gods vanish into legend.

About the Director

Most, if not all, filmmakers are quick to label themselves as “artists,” but in Tarsem Singh’s case, it’s a legitimate branding. A graduate of Pasadena’s Art Center College of Design, the Indian visionary is quick to cite the painters’ influences in all of his movies, and the inspirations shows in bright, eye-stroking manners. For his debut, the phantasmagoric, Jennifer Lopez-starring thriller The Cell, Tarsem layered the flick with trippy imagery, namely the sight of a horse that gets split into pieces by falling glass; with his latest, and only third, feature, Immortals, he sent his mind back to the Renaissance, coloring the film to resemble the works of artist Michelangelo Caravaggio. Watching a Tarsem movie, particularly Immortals, is similar to visiting the Guggenheim and staring at the artwork while tripping on shrooms. And, yes, the experience is as fun as that sounds, even if the film itself isn’t an award-winner.

About the Characters

Theseus: a mortal chosen by Zeus to fight King Hyperion on his evil rampage
King Hyperion: a ruthless and tyrannical king on the quest to discover the Epirus bow
Stavros: a cunning slave and master thief who joins Theseus on this quest,
Phaedra: an oracle who joins Theseus, foretelling the future and guiding Theseus to victory
Zeus: god of the sky and kings of the gods, who tries not to interfere in the matter of mortals
Poseidon: god of the sea, lending a helping hand in the ultimate fight against evil
Athena: goddess of wisdom, giving keen insight and direction the gods
